Here are the main patentable points, distilled to the specific technical elements that carry the novelty. I’ve ordered them by defensibility — strongest claims first — and noted what each one protects against.

Point 1 — Per-weight learnable assignment mask, jointly optimized with codebook parameters under a single reconstruction loss

The method introduces a continuous-relaxed assignment variable, one per weight, that decides which codebook level the weight is reconstructed from. The mask is not precomputed from a sensitivity criterion — it is a learnable parameter updated by gradient descent inside the calibration loop, alongside the codebook projection parameters, under a single block-wise reconstruction loss.

What this protects against: every prior method (AWQ, SqueezeLLM, PTQ1.61, HAWQ, LLM-MQ, OWQ, MatGPTQ, MatQuant) determines the precision assignment either by precomputed sensitivity ranking, deployment-time slicing, or discrete budget search. None of them treat the per-weight assignment as a learnable variable co-optimized with the projections in a unified gradient-based procedure.

Point 2 — Subset-specific projection calibration via co-adaptation of scale and assignment

Each codebook level’s projection parameter is calibrated against only the subset of weights assigned to that level by the mask. The coarse projection sees only coarse-assigned weights; the fine projection sees only fine-assigned weights. The subset and the projection co-adapt during the joint optimization — the projection shapes itself to the subset’s statistics, and the assignment shifts as the projection improves.

What this protects against: MatQuant specifically. MatQuant calibrates each precision’s scale under the assumption that all weights in the tensor are at that precision (uniform-precision optimization repeated per level). This produces different scale values than ours, and the difference is empirically measurable. This is your single sharpest technical differentiator from MatQuant.

Point 3 — Structural coupling of projection parameters across codebook levels via dyadic nesting

The two projection parameters are not independent. They are tied by the factor-of-two step relationship between the coarse and fine codebooks. Gradient updates to one propagate to the other through this structural coupling, so every weight contributes gradient signal to both projections regardless of its current assignment.

What this protects against: the “you just combined multi-precision with scale search” objection. A naive combination of independent multi-precision codebooks with learnable scales produces decoupled scales with ill-posed assignment gradients (because the scales are arithmetically incompatible, so moving a weight across the assignment boundary causes a discontinuity). The nesting is the specific mechanism that makes joint optimization well-posed.

Point 4 — Hierarchical codebook structure as nested sub-lattices under a dyadic step-ratio constraint

The codebooks at successive precision levels form a strict subset hierarchy: every coarse codeword coincides with a fine codeword, the step ratio is exactly two (or a power of two for deeper hierarchies), and the entire structure can be described as nested sub-lattices on the real line.

What this protects against: the “this is just any subset selection” objection. The nesting is not an arbitrary choice — it is the structural condition that enables Points 1, 2, and 3. The dyadic constraint specifically produces the bit-shift relationship between codebook indices that the inference artifact (Point 6) depends on.

Point 5 — Generalization to N-level hierarchies

The same calibration procedure extends to three or more codebook levels, each a strict subset of the next finer level under the dyadic constraint. The mask becomes multi-valued, the projections become one per level, and the joint optimization runs over all of them.

What this protects against: design-arounds using deeper hierarchies (e.g., a competitor doing the same thing across four precision levels). Without this point, a four-level implementation could arguably escape a two-level claim.

Point 6 — Unified single-kernel inference artifact via codebook collapse

The deployed quantized tensor stores all weights as fine-codebook indices, regardless of their assigned level. Weights assigned to the coarse codebook hold fine-codebook indices that correspond to coarse codewords (their low-order bits are structurally fixed). The entire tensor is dequantized through a single kernel and a single memory traversal at inference, with no branching on assignment, no separate buffers, and no accumulation across precision streams.

What this protects against: every prior mixed-precision method whose inference path requires two kernels, two memory traversals, or accumulation. Also covers the deployment artifact independently of the calibration method — useful if a competitor distributes a model that has these structural properties without admitting to the calibration procedure that produced it.

Point 7 — Method operates entirely in the post-training quantization regime

The pre-trained weights are frozen throughout calibration. No task loss is used. No labeled data is used. The calibration set is small and unlabeled. Calibration runs block-by-block with no gradient flow across blocks. Compute and data requirements are at PTQ scale, not QAT scale.

What this protects against: the “this is QAT or a PTQ/QAT hybrid” objection at the patent office. Also distinguishes you from MatQuant (original), which is QAT-based.
